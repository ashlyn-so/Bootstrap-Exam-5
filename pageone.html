The Grid Compass

Source: Niall Kennedy, It boots! | Editing a spreadsheet on the GRiD Compass 1101 | Niall Kennedy | Flickr, 15 March 2006, flickr.com
(https://www.flickr.com/photos/niallkennedy/113345305)

The GRiD compass was the first commercial laptop computer designed in 1979, and released in 1982. Designed by Bill Moggridge, the GRiD compass used a clamshell-style casing which causes the monitor to fold down onto the keyboard. It was about the size of a Large three-ring binder and usually weighed 10 pounds. It originally went on sale in 1982 at the cost of about $8,150 (about $23,000 today).

The latop had an Intel 8086 processor with a 8087 math coprocessor, and 256KB DRAM, 384K bytes bubble memory, electro-luminescent active matrix display, 57 key keyboard, integral 300/1200 bps modem with audio, telephone handset and speaker, RS-232C/RS-422 serial port, IEEE 488 GPIB peripheral interface, GriD link LAN, a clock/calendar with lithium battery back-up, and consumed 60 Watts of power

It came with a text editor, a spreadsheet, a plotter, a terminal emulator, a database management system, and other business software. With these additions, the laptop soon joined with NASA. The first GRiD Compass went into space on November 1983 for the space mission STS-51-G. It's special graphical software displayed the users position corresponding to Earth. It also displayed the line of daylight and darkness. It gained the nickname SPOC (Shuttle Portable On-Board Computer).
